A Necessary Introduction

Top 3 Things People Are Afraid Of According to Psychology Today:

  1. Abandonment
  2. Rejection
  3. Failure

If you really think about it, abandonment, rejection, and failure can all be tied to death in some way or the other--all fears can be.

Death, hands down, is the scariest thing ever, and is singehandedly the easiest way to erase a person's existence from history. However, with the internet, it's easy to leave something behind; it might never be recognized or noticed, but most people don't have any room to complain (except me and Elon Musk, probably). This is going to be the first of hopefully many things I leave behind--a sort of personal conquest, if you will, of my own fears to future readers that will transcend time and centennial differences. I expect future internet archeologists to be amazed.

On the Subject of Immortality

It is scientifically proven that immortality is as mythological as the Philosopher's Stone (and that alchemy is not actually a science). Don't believe me? Check the link: it's hard-core science.

There are organizations who claim they will make immortality a reality, but it's not going to be in this body, nor will that life be as organic and new because it is our body that deteriorates, and it is a dead body that makes a dead person. Instead, we'll probably all become datachips stored in a hunk of metal parts. Which, granted, is better than nothing, but seriously, does that guy look even remotely human to you?

No offense, but I would not want to be him. Or her, for that matter. The purpose of immortality is to stay alive (and preferably young) forever in the body that you have. Storing yourself in a computer or robot isn't that. And though some people may argue that we can probably design the features of a robot to very closely match your own, it's probably not going to look the same--or downright creepy due to the Uncanny Valley effect.

Because we are humans and like to think of ourselves as having some divine purpose above simple reproduction like all other lifeforms, it's difficult to wrap our heads around the fact that, maybe, we're just like everything else that lives: we are life, but we are not eternal, and perhaps life--consciousness, to be more exact--is an illusion we paint for ourselves on an old canvas in danger of crumbling.

I am definitely not an exception; I'm making this webpage, after all. I'm not incredibly optimistic though. I think that it is more than likely that our (G-g)ods, heavens, and afterlives (or rebirth if you're into that sort of thing) are figments of our warped reality, a manefestation of our yearning hope that we are not disposible or easily forgettable. That's why I'm making this webpage.

Why I Am Obsessed With Having A Legacy
"Countries are mere vessels of granfaloonism. Petty little threads that meaninglessly pretend to tie humans together when in fact they drive humans further apart. A single world must be created under my rule where all are truly equal: an empire of infinite wealth and power. That is the way."
- Your Future Emperor

I once chatted with a friend over what it would be like to take over the world, and this is a quote I directly copied from the text. (If you want to learn more about your future emperor, click here.) I'm not really ambitious in that way (I'd rather explore space. If I got to Mars first, can you image all the press coverage I'd get? Neil Armstrong wouldn't be able to touch me.), but he's got the dream. It's certainly one way to leave your mark on the world.

Legacy. It transcends stardom in every way because it extends to the future. Who in the world will remember Taylor Swift a hundred years from now? Maybe they'll remember some blond chick who made music (or movies, who knows), but really. With all her successes today, you'd expect better. But Leonardo da Vinci? The guy died in 1519 and still, his name is praised today. Scientist, artist, architect, engineer, the works. That's a legacy.

Legacy is the alternative, the other way to preserve a part of ourselves onto this plane (whatever it may be, I'm not philsophical enough to guess). It's something that we leave behind. Legacy is not immortality--we've established that that's not quite realistic--but it's something.

The Importance of Choice and Decisions

I have included MIT's curriculum guide. This thing links to the five departments, which link to more stuff. It is a representation of choice: the act of selecting or making a decision when faced with two or more possibilities (Google).

I don't know whether choices can define who we are (as half the time they're made with one eye closed), but I can confirm how important it is to make them because inevitably, it is our choices that will be remembered, much more than our personalities or who we were as people. Actions speak louder than words and character, I suppose.

So how can we make good decisions, because that is the ultimate question, isn't it? You'd expect this entire ramble to end in a glorious fireshower of wisdom, or the secret--genie's lamp, if you will--to finally be presented to you. But that's not the point at all. The truth is, this entire rambling does lead up to this point, but not to form any conclusions since I don't have any. Again, I'll remind you the purpose is to preserve my legacy, not to educate you since I doubt I could--things have probably changed a hundred years in the future.

However, it would probably be good to try anyway, to leave some sort of impression on you because that's what writers do. I can't just be remembered for being me, can I? Actions speak louder than words and character.

The first thing to understand about choice is that there are varying levels of importance. Meaning, some decisions are more important than others. You'd be surprised how many people don't know this. Here is where I tell you to choose your priorities: a very important choice that will define all that you do for much of your life (undoubtably one of the more high-stakes choices). The rest of your important choices will depend on this. What do you like? What do you want? Such generic questions will help you get to the specifics.

Next, you must be willing to make mistakes. You will mess up. That is a fact that will never change. What's important is that you:

  1. Learn
  2. Never do it again

Perhaps what deserves most emphasis, however, is taking risks. The beauty of choice is that there is no "right"--you can never make a wrong choice. Your decisions can certainly lead to negative consequences, but they're not necessarily wrong (because the other paths could be just as bad, though we will never know). Life is a risk, as you probably have heard. The safest option is, inevitably, death, because it is the only certainty that will yield the same results every time. Everything uncertain is a risk, but it's the only way to move forward.

Your success lies in the success of your choices. Your legacy depends on how much of an impact your choices make. This, invariably, is the secret, I believe. I could be wrong, but this is my take. (Frankly, I don't think I'm wrong. It's not that complicated, just hard to accomplish.)
